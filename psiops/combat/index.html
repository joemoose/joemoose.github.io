    Combat¶

    Actions¶

    In a single round of combat, your character can spend up to two Action Points (AP). AP indicates the maximum number of actions you can take during a round of combat. You can spend your AP on a variety of actions (such as movement, attacks, interacting with objects in the zone, activating explosives, using a piece of gear) in any order you choose (such as move, then attack; attack, then move; attack twice, or move twice, etc.). Once you use all your AP, you’re done for the round.

    Initiative¶

    When combat breaks out, a turn order must first be established for all the combatants. Turns allow every player to participate (no hogging the limelight) and provide tactical opportunities during combat.

    Step 1: Declaring Actions¶

    At the beginning of every round, the GM describes the actions likely taken by your enemies. All players then declare their character’s intentions for the round before rolling any dice.

    Step 2: Determining Fast and Slow Rounds¶

    Next, determine whether the declared actions involve a fast or slow round.

    • A fast round consists of spending only one action point.
    • A slow round consists of spending two actions points.

    Step 3: Setting Turn Order and Resolving Actions¶

    After determining whether all combatants take either fast or slow rounds, the order for resolving actions is as follows:

    1. Resolve all fast rounds before slow rounds.
    2. Melee attacks occur before ranged attacks.
    3. If players and opponents both act in the same fast round, the player must make a DEX or WIL save to act before the opponents. Otherwise, they act after the opponents.
    4. Resolve all slow rounds.
    5. If players and opponents both act in the same slow round, the player must make a DEX or WIL save to act before the opponents. Otherwise, they act after the opponents.

    Example

    For example, a player and their opponent attack each other using a fast round (single action) with combat knives (melee). The player must make a DEX or WIL save to attack first. The player would have gone first automatically if the enemy had used a gun (ranged) or taken two actions (slow round).

    Note

    A new initiative order is determined at the beginning of every round of combat.

    Tips

    Use fast rounds tactically. If the GM indicates your opponent will take a slow round, you can act first with a single decisive action as a fast round.

    Stance¶

    With an action, you can change your character’s stance between standing, crouching, or prone.

    The closer to the ground you are, the more accurate your ranged attacks.

    Stance Modifier
    Standing as normal
    Crouching Gain advantage on ranged attack damage and disadvantage on any ranged damage received.
    Prone As crouch, and also +1D6 to attack damage and -1D6 to attack damage received.

    Note

    Melee attacks reverse these modifiers; for example, you take a disadvantage on attack damage rolls and suffer an advantage on melee damage received when crouching.

    Area Fire¶

    In most firefights, the targets aren’t simply standing in plain sight, firing away at each other. You’ll generally attack an area you suspect contains a concealed enemy.

    In an infantry battle, soldiers fire at a location from which they are receiving fire, referred to as area fire.

    This approach differs slightly from traditional RPGS, with attackers declaring specific targets and rolling whether they hit.

    Area fire is directed at an entire zone, called the beaten zone, rather than individual targets. All of your squad should add to the volume of fire directed at the beaten zone, piling on automatic fire and explosives to maximise the effect.

    Only when an enemy becomes visible with a clear line of sight, generally by moving in the open or being unaware of an enemy, does a soldier engage in more accurate direct fire.

    Resolving Area fire¶

    You will most likely not know the effect your area fire has had. If the enemy stops firing, are they shot, or did they withdraw? You can’t be sure until you enter the zone and investigate. Of course, moving forward might expose you to direct fire from any enemy still lying low in the zone. In practice, you’ll want to continue pounding the area for a few combat rounds.

    Behind the scenes, your GM will secretly resolve the effects of your area fire and any damage to enemies within the beaten zone.

    In short, any concealed target in the beaten zone has a 1 in 6 chance of being hit and suffering one point of damage for every D6 attack damage of area fire piled onto the zone. The more damage piled into a beaten zone, the greater the chance of hitting concealed enemies and the greater the damage.

    Direct-Fire Attacks¶

    Direct-fire attacks always hit. If you have a clear line of sight to a spotted target, roll your weapon’s damage dice + weapon skill and do that much damage to an enemy minus their armour rating.

    Tip

    Remember, this is also true for your enemies; their direct-fire attacks always hit you.

    Critical Hits¶

    Rolling maximum damage on a weapon’s die roll is a critical hit. A critical hit ignores the target’s armour rating.

    Cover¶

    If a target is behind hard cover, the cover functions as armour, reducing damage from direct fire. The GM will let you know, but generally, cover reduces damage between 2 and 12 points.

    Enhanced Attacks¶

    When an attack is enhanced, such as attacking an unaware enemy, add 2D6 to your total damage.

    Impaired Attacks¶

    When an attack is impaired, such as fighting while your hands are bound, subtract 2D6 from your total damage.

    Dual Wielder¶

    If attacking with two weapons, roll damage for both and use the best result.

    Multiple Attackers¶

    If multiple combatants attack the same target with direct fire, roll damage for each attacker, but use only the best result. Add +1 damage to the total for each attacker after the first.

    Blast¶

    Attacks with the blast quality affect all targets in a zone, rolling damage separately for each affected combatant. Blast refers to anything from explosives or grenades to sweeping fire from machine guns. If unsure how many targets can be affected in the zone, roll the weapon’s damage die for the number of targets affected.

    Defending¶

    If you take an action to focus exclusively on defending, you can make a DEX save to block, parry, or dodge subsequent attacks and avoid any damage. You can’t defend against blast damage or psionic attacks.

    A critical success on your defence save allows you to counterattack your enemy immediately.

    Defence ends once you take some other action.

    Gambits¶

    Gambits are combat manoeuvres such as stunning, shoving, disarming, or tripping an enemy. Gambits don’t cause damage directly but may indirectly (for example, pushing an enemy off a ledge). They’re resolved with a relevant attribute save.

    Tip

    A clever gambit might end a battle before it even begins. Combat isn't a sport. Fight dirty!

    Suffering a Gamit¶

    Clever enemies will try to use gambits on you. If so, the GM gives you a choice on a failed save: either suffer the effect of the gambit or take damage from the attack.

    Advantage in Combat¶

    Characters can gain an advantage in combat by attacking an enemy who’s on lower ground, off balance, distracted, or tactically disadvantaged in any significant way.

    When a character has an advantage against an enemy, you can apply your advantage to a damage roll, gambit attempt, or an attribute save against that enemy, such as when defending.

    Tip

    If you find yourself at a disadvantage in combat, your enemies will exploit their advantage to the fullest. Consider a withdrawal or try new tactics.

    Aiming¶

    If you take an action to first aim at a target, damage on your subsequent attacks is enhanced. If you’re attacked in the same round as aiming, your aim is disrupted, and your damage is impaired.

    Subdual¶

    Rather than inflicting grievous harm, you can attempt to subdue an enemy using a melee weapon. Ranged weapons can’t subdue. Declare that you’re trying to subdue and attack as usual, but halve the damage after reducing it by armour rating. An enemy suffering critical damage or death from a subdual attack is knocked unconscious.

    Morale¶

    When you’re fighting a group of enemies, the enemies must pass a WIL save to continue fighting after they take their first casualty and again after they lose half their numbers or their leader is defeated. Enemies who fail morale immediately surrender or rout and attempt to flee.

    Lone foes must check morale when reduced to 0 HP.

    Some fanatical or mindless enemies might not be required to check morale—they’ll fight to the bitter end.

    Note

    Morale doesn’t apply to players. You have to decide when it’s prudent to run away!

    Disengaging¶

    If you want to retreat to an adjacent zone while an enemy occupies your zone, you must first make a successful STR or DEX save to disengage from the fight. If you fail, the enemy makes a free attack against you as you retreat from the zone.

    Detachments¶

    When you battle a group of identical enemies, such as a squad of mercenaries, each with the same attributes and weapons, the GM may combine the enemies into a single abstract unit or detachment. A detachment has the same attributes, HP, armour rating, and damage dice as a single unit member.

    Attacks by individuals against a detachment are impaired unless they have the blast quality. Attacks by a detachment against an individual are enhanced and deal blast damage. Attacks by a detachment cannot be dodged or parried while defending.

    When a detachment takes critical damage, it immediately fails morale and routs. If a detachment is reduced to zero Strength or Dexterity, it’s destroyed.
