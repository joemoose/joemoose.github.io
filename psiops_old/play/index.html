    How to Play¶

    Introduction¶

    The core of this game is a conversation between the players and the game master (GM). The GM describes the players’ current situation. Then, the players ask questions and tell the GM what their characters do. The GM explains the results of their actions, and then the players react again and describe more activities, and so on.

    The GM answers your questions and always respects your freedom of choice. The GM provides challenges, obstacles, and situations to solve and overcome. Still, the game is more than just a story narrated by the GM—it’s an interactive narrative built on your and your fellow players’ decisions. Consider it as both creating and acting in an adventure story simultaneously.

    Suppose the outcome of your character’s actions isn’t immediately apparent, or the activity is risky. In that case, the GM might ask you to roll dice to determine an outcome.

    Safety¶

    Everyone at the table, players and GM, should feel safe exploring themes, characters, and storylines that might fall outside everyday topics of conversation. GMs and players should discuss what themes or topics will be in the game, won't ever be in the game, and might be in the game but only in passing.

    Everyone at the table must feel comfortable saying and allowing others to say “pause” to take a break, “rewind” to revisit a previous situation or decision, and, most importantly, “stop” without fear of judgment if something in the conversation or game makes them uncomfortable. If a “stop” is called, drop that topic from the game and steer the narrative to a different situation; don't debate its merits.

    Best Practices¶

    • Engage and collaborate with your GM and other players. Be kind. Be positive. Be authentic.
    • Ask lots of questions. Gather information. Talk to NPCs. Make notes. Draw maps.
    • Dice are dangerous. Solve problems with planning and ingenuity, not dice rolls. Think laterally. Always gain an advantage. Your character sheet or skills don’t limit you.
    • Foes are deadly. Combat is not a sport. Fight dirty. Sometimes, you need to retreat.

    • Play to win, but savour a loss. Leave your mark on the world. Create a memorable story.

      Quote

      Play your character like you’re driving a stolen car.
      –John Harper, Blades in the Dark

    On to the mechanics!

    Attribute Saves¶

    When you describe your character doing something risky or with an uncertain outcome, the GM may ask you to make an attribute save to determine whether you succeed. The GM will describe possible complications or outcomes to help in your decision-making.

    To make an attribute save…

    Grab as many D6 equal to the relevant attribute DM plus additional dice equal to an applicable skill level, if any. These dice form your base dice pool. Roll all the dice in your pool.

    • SUCCESS: To succeed with your action, you must roll at least one 6. A 6 is a success without any complications. If you roll multiple 6s, you achieve a critical success.
    • PARTIAL SUCCESS: If the highest die is a 5, you succeed, but with a complication.
    • FAILURE: If the highest dice is a 4 or less, you fail at the task and suffer a setback.

    Notes

    • Given sufficient time, relevant skill or training, and the proper equipment, your actions will generally always succeed—the GM won't call for an attribute save.
    • However, if one of these three requirements is missing, that would require an attribute save to determine an outcome.
    • The action would be impossible if two or more of these requirements were missing. It's time to think of a new approach!

    Using Attributes¶

    Your character is a determined, resourceful special forces trooper capable of doing almost anything. So, when the outcome of an action is risky, and the GM calls for an attribute save to determine an outcome, how do we decide which attribute to roll against?

    Your description of your character’s actions determines the applicable attribute. Consider a heavy, locked bunker door—how do you pass through it?

    • If you try and force the door open, that’s a STR save.
    • If you use a lock pick to unlock the door, that’s a DEX save.
    • If you want to bribe a guard to open it, that’s a WIL save.
    • If you consult a schematic to bypass the locking mechanism, that's an EDU save.

    Tip

    Don’t just think about what your character is capable of based on their skills and attributes, but rather how you would approach a problem. Use all of your character’s resources.

    Dice Pool Modifiers¶

    Depending on the narrative situation, the GM might assign modifiers to your save that add or remove dice from your pool.

    • A +1 modifier means you add one extra die to your dice pool, +2 means you add two extra dice, and so on.
    • A −1 modifier means you remove one die from your dice pool, −2 means two fewer, and so on.

    Several modifiers can apply to the same roll and are cumulative.

    Note

    After adding or subtracting all modifiers, if you have zero dice or less in your pool, roll 2D6 and take the lower result instead.

    Critical Success¶

    If you roll two or more 6s on your attribute save, you reach your stated goal and gain some additional bonus effect, depending on the situation and the skill used. You can suggest a bonus effect yourself. The GM has the final say. Some suggestions:

    • You act quickly.
    • You act quietly.
    • You do more damage.
    • You impress someone.
    • You help another character.
    • You discover something unexpected.

    Vantages¶

    The primary way to modify your saves is through vantages: favourable situations in the narrative beyond what might be considered typical have an advantage and a +1 DM. In comparison, unfavourable or inherently difficult situations might have a disadvantage and a -1 DM.

    Tip

    Always look to gain an advantage over enemies and situations!

    Pushing Your Roll¶

    Your initial save roll reflects a controlled action. Suppose you fail your initial roll or want to add additional successes. In that case, you can lean into the action, giving it everything you’ve got and pushing yourself to the limit.

    This is called pushing the roll and lets you re-roll any dice that don’t show the result of 1. A result of 1 is always a fail and can never be re-rolled when pushing. After a push, you cannot change back to the previous result. All dice count as rolled after the push.

    You can push a save only once. If you fail on your second try, you are stuck dealing with the consequences.

    Cost of Pushing: Stress¶

    Pushing always comes with a risk or a cost. When you push a roll, you immediately take one stress point for each die you re-roll.

    All stress points are removed after resting for a shift (6 hours) in a safe location.

    Note: Per the GM, other situations in the narrative, such as horrific events, may also add stress to your character.

    Pushed Rolls¶

    After marking your stress and re-rolling the pushed dice, each result of 6 counts as a success, meaning the built-up stress increases your chances to succeed, as it makes you more alert. However, if you roll a 1 on one or more pushed dice, you trigger a panic check or possibly an unwanted effect.

    Panic Check¶

    Roll D6+STRESS and consult the following table for a possible panic effect.

    D6+STRESS Result
    1–6 Keeping it Together. You manage to stay focused.
    7 Drop Item. You drop a weapon or other important item—the GM decides which one.
    8 Nervous Twitch. You temporarily lose one point of Willpower. Starting one hour after the last loss of Willpower, you recover one point of Willpower per hour.
    9 Tremble. You tremble uncontrollably. All saves using DEX suffer a -1 DM.
    10 Freeze. You're frozen by fear or stress for one round, losing your next turn.
    11 Seek Cover. You must use your next action to avoid immediate danger and find a safe spot. If you have an enemy in your zone, you must make a retreat roll. You lose one stress point, but all other characters nearby gain one stress. After one round, you can act normally.
    12 Scream. You scream for one round, losing your next turn. You lose one stress point, but every character who hears you scream must make an immediate panic check.
    13 Flee. You must flee to a safe place and refuse to leave it for D6 rounds. You won't attack anyone and won't attempt anything dangerous. You lose one stress point, but every character who sees you flee makes a panic check.
    14 Berserk. You must immediately attack the nearest target, friendly or not. You won't stop until you or the target is down. Every character who witnesses your rampage must make an immediate panic roll.
    15+ Catatonic. You collapse to the floor and can't talk or move, staring blankly into oblivion. Every character who witnesses your fall must make an immediate panic roll.

    Opposed Saves¶

    If two opponents attempt to overcome the other, whoever is most at risk should make the attribute save.

    Assistance¶

    Allies can assist another player in an attribute save whenever feasible. The player making the save and all those coming to their aid make individual attribute saves; if a majority succeeds, the save is a success. Note that everyone involved suffers the potential consequences if the save fails.

    Group Saves¶

    When everyone in the adventuring party tries to do the same thing, such as sneaking past a guard, the GM will ask that the character most likely to fail makes the attribute save.

    Time¶

    Gameplay measures time in three durations:

    Unit Duration Primary Use
    Round 6 seconds Combat
    Turn 6 minutes Exploration
    Shift 6 hours Travel

    Distance¶

    The game uses zones as abstractions of distance and area to divide larger physical spaces into smaller units, like rooms in a house.

    For example, at the start of combat, the GM divides the battlefield into at least three zones: one where the party starts, one where the enemies start, and the area between them.

    The distance within and between zones is relative:

    Close: Within the same zone.

    • You can touch it.
    • Most actions affect things in your zone.
    • You can speak softly and be heard.
    • In range of melee attacks.

    Near: One zone away.

    • You can get to it.
    • You can speak and be heard.
    • In range of thrown weapons.

    Far: Two zones away.

    • You can shoot at it.
    • You can shout and be heard.
    • In range for short-range weapons.

    Distant: Three zones away.

    • You can see it.
    • Too far away for verbal communication.
    • In range for long-range weapons.

    Zone Features¶

    Zones can have various features which affect actions performed within them. Here are some examples:

    Cluttered: The zone is covered by dense undergrowth or filled with debris of some sort. You must make a DEX save when you move into the zone. Failure means you manage to get into the zone, but you fall.

    Dimly Lit: The zone is dimly lit. Saves in the zone gets a −1 modifier. Ranged attacks into the zone also suffer a −1 modifier and can’t pass through the zone.

    Cramped: A crawlspace or narrow tunnel. In a cramped zone, you can only crawl, not run. You also cannot move or shoot past individuals in front of you or against targets behind them.

    Borders and Line of Sight¶

    A wall or other obstruction can block the border between two adjacent zones. A blocked border can have a door or opening, allowing movement between the zones.

    A blocked border generally blocks the line of sight even if there is a door or opening in it—unless you’re standing adjacent to the doorway and peeking through.

    Open borders don’t block vision or movement.
